Visions in Wood: The Living Tradition of Sri Lankan Mask Art

Sri Lankan mask art is a vivid expression of the island’s rich cultural history—a unique confluence of religious belief, ritual performance, ancestral tradition, and folk creativity. Rooted in centuries-old practices, these masks are not mere decorative objects; they are part of an intricate cultural grammar through which communities have confronted disease, invoked divine intervention, narrated epics, and expressed the unseen. From the coastal rituals of the south to contemporary art galleries, the Sri Lankan mask tradition remains a vital cultural force that bridges the sacred and the aesthetic.Mask-making in Sri Lanka finds its roots in animistic and shamanistic traditions, long before the arrival of Buddhism on the island. The southern coastal regions, particularly Ambalangoda, have historically been the cultural heartland of this art form. Influenced by indigenous beliefs, Hindu practices, and Buddhist cosmology, Sri Lankan masks evolved into a highly stylized and codified tradition associated with three main categories: Kolam, Sanni Yakuma, and Raksha.Each category serves a distinct purpose.
