A Dance With Darkness

By  :  Chamathka Kaveen Bandara

Published : 2nd April 2025

Some films linger with you not because of what they say, but what they leave unsaid. Lester James Peries' 1972 masterwork "Nidanaya" (The Treasure) is such a film, a haunting meditation on greed that unfolds with the inexorable logic of classical tragedy. It is cinema as ritual, a solemn procession toward doom that knows its destination from the first frame but takes its time getting there, allowing us to feel the weight of each footstep. Set in rural Sri Lanka during the early 20th century, "Nidanaya" tells the story of Willy Abenayake, a wealthy landowner whose discovery of an ancient palm leaf manuscript sets in motion a chain of events that will lead to murder and suicide. The manuscript describes a hidden treasure that can only be accessed by sacrificing a virgin woman named Irene with four black birthmarks on her neck. When Willy encounters a young woman who bears these exact marks, he courts and marries her, all the while planning her death.


The film opens with an air of quiet foreboding, as Willy Abenayake, a man of privilege and superstition, stumbles upon the ola leaf manuscript that will consume his every thought. The ancient text, brittle with age, speaks of an unimaginable fortune hidden within a rock cave, a treasure once belonging to a forgotten king. Yet, the manuscript’s conditions are as chilling as they are precise: the treasure can only be claimed through the ritualistic sacrifice of a virgin woman marked by four distinct black birthmarks on her neck. This revelation ignites a slow-burning obsession in Willy, one that will warp his morality and lead him down a path of calculated deception.


Willy’s fateful encounter with Irene by the riverbank is framed with an eerie inevitability. The camera lingers on her neck, where the four birthmarks—dark as ink against her skin—confirm that she is the one described in the manuscript. From this moment, Willy’s courtship is not one of love, but of predation. He gathers information about her, ensuring she meets the manuscript’s exact specifications: young, untouched, and marked by destiny. His proposal of marriage is not a romantic gesture, but a strategic move, a means to bring her closer to the sacrificial altar without arousing suspicion.


As husband and wife, Willy and Irene inhabit a world of unspoken tension. Irene, innocent and devoted, notices her husband’s distant demeanor, his constant preoccupation with thoughts she cannot decipher. When she finally gathers the courage to ask him what weighs so heavily on his mind, Willy seizes the opportunity to manipulate her trust. He speaks of a religious custom, a sacred duty that must be performed at a nearby rock shrine. Irene, eager to please her husband and unaware of the horror that awaits her, agrees without hesitation. Her compliance is heartbreaking, a testament to her unwavering faith in the man who has already condemned her.


The journey to the rock cave is steeped in dread, each step bringing Irene closer to her doom. The landscape itself seems to recoil at what is about to transpire, the wind whispering warnings she cannot hear. Willy, his face a mask of grim determination, performs the ritual with methodical precision. The moment of sacrifice is not depicted with graphic violence, but with unbearable stillness—the act itself is almost ceremonial, as though Willy believes he is fulfilling some higher purpose rather than committing cold-blooded murder. Irene’s death is swift, her trust betrayed in the most unforgivable way.


Yet, the treasure remains elusive. The cave yields nothing, the manuscript’s promise revealed as a cruel illusion. Willy’s realization is a slow, suffocating horror—he has taken an innocent life for nothing. The weight of his sin crashes upon him, and in the aftermath, he returns home a broken man. His diary becomes his confessional, the pages filling with the entire sordid tale, as though writing it down might somehow absolve him. But there is no absolution to be found. The final act of his tragedy is his suicide, a desperate escape from the guilt that has hollowed him out. The rope around his neck is both punishment and poetic justice, a mirror of the noose he placed around Irene’s life.


"Nidanaya" is a film that thrives in the spaces between actions, in the quiet moments where dread takes root. Willy’s descent into moral ruin is not marked by grand gestures, but by small, deliberate choices—each one leading him further from humanity. Irene, in her innocence, becomes a sacrificial lamb to his avarice, her death a futile offering to gods or greed that were never listening. The film’s power lies in its restraint, in the way it allows the horror to unfold with the quiet certainty of a nightmare. There are no last-minute reprieves, no divine interventions—only the devastating consequences of a man who believed treasure was worth more than a human life.


The tragedy of "Nidanaya" is not just in the lives lost, but in the futility of it all. Willy’s quest ends in emptiness, his hands stained with blood but no richer for it. The treasure, if it ever existed, was never meant to be found—or perhaps the true curse was the greed that drove men to seek it. The film leaves us with haunting questions about the cost of obsession and the lies we tell ourselves to justify the unforgivable. In the end, Willy’s diary is less a record of his crimes than a epitaph for his soul, a final admission that some treasures are better left buried.


Every frame of "Nidanaya" is heavy with symbolism, from the ominous manuscript that crumbles like morality itself to the birthmarks that seal Irene’s fate. The rock cave, a gaping maw in the earth, becomes a metaphor for the abyss within Willy’s soul—a darkness that consumes everything it touches. Even the act of writing, which should be an act of creation, becomes one of destruction in Willy’s hands, his words etching his guilt into permanence. The film’s pacing mirrors the inevitability of a folktale, where every step feels predestined, every choice leading toward an ending that cannot be escaped.


Irene’s character, though doomed from the start, lingers in the mind long after the film ends. Her trust in Willy is absolute, her love genuine, and it is this very purity that makes her fate so devastating. There is a profound cruelty in the way Willy exploits her devotion, twisting it into a weapon against her. Her final moments, unknowing and unguarded, are a stark contrast to Willy’s calculated brutality. In another story, she might have been the heroine; here, she is merely a means to an end, a casualty of a man’s unraveling sanity.


Willy’s suicide is the only conclusion the story could have, a final act of self-destruction that underscores the film’s themes of futility and despair. His death is not redemption, but surrender—an admission that the treasure he sought was never worth the price he paid. The rope around his neck is a fitting end for a man who thought he could bargain with fate, only to discover that some debts cannot be repaid. The diary he leaves behind is both confession and curse, a testament to the ruin that greed brings.


"Nidanaya" does not offer catharsis. There is no lesson learned, no justice served—only the bleak aftermath of a man’s hubris. The film’s brilliance lies in its refusal to soften the blow, to offer hope where none exists. It is a story about the consequences of believing that some lives are expendable in the pursuit of wealth, and the chilling truth that such beliefs only lead to ruin. By the time the credits roll, the audience is left with the weight of what they’ve witnessed—a tragedy not of chance, but of choice, where every step toward damnation was taken willingly.


In the end, "Nidanaya" is more than a cautionary tale about greed; it is a mirror held up to the darkest corners of human nature. Willy’s journey is one of dehumanization, where a woman becomes a means to an end, where love is feigned for the sake of profit, and where morality is discarded in the name of superstition. The film’s power comes from its simplicity—there are no twists, no last-minute revelations, only the slow, crushing realization that some paths lead only to darkness. It is a masterpiece of understated horror, a reminder that the greatest monsters are not those who lurk in shadows, but those who walk in daylight, their sins hidden behind a mask of normalcy.
